How to Troubleshoot and Maintain Your Water Heater
====================================================

A water heater typically lasts 8–12 years. Many common problems can be diagnosed at home, though repairs often require a licensed plumber — especially for gas heaters or electrical work.

TYPES OF WATER HEATERS
------------------------
- Tank water heater (most common): stores 30–80 gallons of hot water; heated by gas burner or electric elements
- Tankless (on-demand): heats water as needed; more efficient but more complex to service
- Heat pump water heater: very efficient; extracts heat from the air

This guide focuses on traditional tank water heaters.

NO HOT WATER
-------------
For gas heaters:
1. Check that the gas supply valve is open (handle parallel to the pipe).
2. Look through the pilot window — is the pilot light on?
3. To relight a pilot: set the control knob to PILOT, press and hold it, use a long lighter to ignite at the pilot opening, keep holding for 30–60 seconds after ignition, then release slowly.
4. If the pilot won't stay lit, the thermocouple (safety sensor) is likely faulty. Replacement cost: $20–$50 in parts; a plumber can do this in under an hour.
5. If the gas supply is confirmed and the pilot won't light at all, call a professional — there may be a gas supply issue.

For electric heaters:
1. Check your breaker box — reset the breaker if it tripped.
2. Many electric heaters have a high-temperature reset button near the thermostat (under an access panel). Press it once.
3. If water is still cold after 2 hours, one or both heating elements may have burned out. Replacement requires draining the tank and is best handled by a plumber.

NOT ENOUGH HOT WATER
---------------------
1. Thermostat setting: Recommended setting is 120°F (49°C). Too low = lukewarm water. Too high = scalding risk.
   - Gas: adjust the dial on the gas valve directly.
   - Electric: turn off the breaker, remove the access panels, adjust the thermostat behind the insulation.
2. Tank undersized for demand: Consider a larger tank or tankless heater.
3. Sediment buildup (see Maintenance section below).
4. Failing heating element: bottom element often fails first in electric heaters.

RUSTY OR DISCOLORED WATER
--------------------------
Brown or rusty water indicates one of two issues:
- Corroding sacrificial anode rod: the rod inside the tank protects the tank from rust. It needs replacement every 3–5 years.
- Tank itself is corroding: this means the heater is near end-of-life and should be replaced. Do not ignore this — a corroding tank can fail and flood your home.

POPPING OR RUMBLING NOISE
--------------------------
Sediment (mineral deposits) builds up on the bottom of the tank over time and causes noises as water bubbles through it. Flushing the tank annually prevents this.

Flush procedure:
1. Turn off the heater (gas: set to PILOT; electric: flip the breaker).
2. Connect a garden hose to the drain valve at the bottom of the tank.
3. Run the other end to a floor drain or outside.
4. Open a hot water tap inside to prevent vacuum lock.
5. Open the drain valve and let the tank empty.
6. Open the cold water supply briefly to stir up remaining sediment, then drain again.
7. Close the drain valve, remove the hose, let the tank refill, then restart the heater.
Frequency: annually in areas with hard water; every 2 years elsewhere.

ANODE ROD REPLACEMENT
-----------------------
The anode rod is a magnesium or aluminum rod that corrodes in place of the tank. Replacing it every 3–5 years dramatically extends tank life.
1. Turn off the heater and cold water supply. Open a hot water tap to reduce pressure.
2. Locate the anode rod — usually on top of the heater, sometimes under the sheet metal top panel.
3. Use a 1-1/16 inch socket and breaker bar to unscrew it.
4. Inspect: if it's less than 1/2 inch in diameter or heavily corroded, replace it.
5. Wrap threads with Teflon tape, install the new rod, and tighten.

TEMPERATURE AND PRESSURE RELIEF VALVE
----------------------------------------
The T&P valve is a safety device that opens if pressure or temperature becomes dangerously high.
- Test annually: lift the lever briefly and let it snap back. Water should discharge into the drain pipe.
- If it drips continuously, it needs replacement (about $20, straightforward for a plumber).
- Never cap or remove the T&P valve — this is a serious safety hazard.

WHEN TO CALL A PROFESSIONAL
-----------------------------
- Gas smell near the heater (leave the house and call your gas company)
- Water leaking from the tank body (not fittings) — tank must be replaced
- Electric wiring issues or no power after resetting breaker
- Tankless heater error codes
- Any repair on a heater still under warranty (DIY may void it)

EXPECTED LIFESPAN AND REPLACEMENT
-----------------------------------
- Tank heater: 8–12 years. Signs it's time to replace: rust in water, pooling water at the base, frequent repairs, age over 10 years.
