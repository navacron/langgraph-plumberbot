Drain Cleaning Tips and Maintenance Guide
==========================================

Clogged drains are the most common plumbing problem homeowners face. Most clogs can be prevented with good habits and cleared with simple tools. Here's what you need to know.

WHAT CAUSES DRAINS TO CLOG
----------------------------
- Kitchen drains: grease, food particles, coffee grounds
- Bathroom drains: hair, soap scum, toothpaste buildup
- Toilet drains: non-flushable items (wipes, cotton balls, dental floss)
- Floor drains: sediment, cleaning product residue
- Main sewer line: tree root intrusion, grease buildup, deteriorated pipes

DIY CLEARING METHODS
---------------------

Method 1 — Boiling water flush (for grease and soap)
Best for: kitchen sinks with early grease buildup
1. Boil a full kettle of water.
2. Pour it slowly down the drain in 2–3 stages, allowing the hot water to work between pours.
3. Effective for dissolving soap and light grease deposits.
Caution: Do not use boiling water on PVC pipes that may be older or poorly rated — use very hot tap water instead.

Method 2 — Baking soda and white vinegar
Best for: mild clogs and regular maintenance
1. Pour 1/2 cup of baking soda into the drain.
2. Follow immediately with 1/2 cup of white vinegar.
3. Cover the drain opening with a stopper or cloth to force the reaction downward.
4. Let it fizz for 15–30 minutes.
5. Flush with a kettle of very hot water.
Repeat monthly as a preventive treatment.

Method 3 — Plunger (cup plunger for sinks, flange plunger for toilets)
Best for: fully blocked drains
1. Fill the sink with 2–3 inches of water to create a seal.
2. Cover the overflow opening (on bathroom sinks) with a wet cloth to maintain suction.
3. Place the plunger cup firmly over the drain.
4. Pump vigorously 10–15 times, then pull away sharply.
5. Repeat until water drains freely.

Method 4 — Manual drain snake (hand auger)
Best for: clogs deeper in the pipe (beyond the P-trap)
1. Insert the flexible cable into the drain opening.
2. Crank the handle clockwise as you push the cable deeper.
3. When you hit resistance, crank vigorously to break up or hook the clog.
4. Pull the cable back slowly while continuing to crank.
5. Run water to flush the loosened material.

Available at hardware stores for $20–$40. A worthwhile investment for any homeowner.

WHAT NOT TO USE
----------------
Chemical drain cleaners (e.g., Drano, Liquid-Plumr):
- Contain caustic acids or lye that can damage PVC pipes, corrode metal pipes, and ruin rubber seals.
- Dangerous to skin and eyes; toxic fumes.
- Can make a clog harder to remove professionally (the chemical sits on top of the clog and makes it slippery but doesn't fully clear it).
- If you must use them, do so only once and follow immediately with professional snaking.

Never pour down any drain:
- Cooking grease or oil (solidifies in pipes)
- Coffee grounds (accumulate in bends)
- Pasta, rice, or bread (expand with water)
- Eggshells (sharp particles damage pipes)
- Flushable wipes, paper towels, cotton balls, hair
- Paint, solvents, or chemicals (environmental and pipe hazard)

KITCHEN DRAIN MAINTENANCE
---------------------------
- Use a sink strainer to catch food particles.
- Run cold water while operating a garbage disposal — cold water keeps grease solid so it can be chopped and flushed.
- After each use of the disposal, let cold water run for an additional 30 seconds.
- Once a month: put a few ice cubes and a handful of coarse salt in the disposal and run it — scours the grinding chamber.
- Flush with boiling water monthly to clear grease buildup.

BATHROOM DRAIN MAINTENANCE
----------------------------
- Install a hair catcher over the drain — the single most effective prevention tool.
- Clean the catcher after every shower.
- Monthly: pour a cup of baking soda, wait 30 minutes, flush with hot water.
- Every 3–6 months: remove and clean the pop-up drain stopper (it collects soap scum and hair).

OUTDOOR AND FLOOR DRAINS
--------------------------
- Check outdoor drains seasonally for leaves, dirt, and debris.
- Pour water down floor drains in infrequently used areas quarterly to keep the P-trap filled (prevents sewer gas from entering).
- Mark and protect outdoor cleanout caps — these are the access points plumbers use for main line snaking.

WHEN IT'S A MAIN LINE PROBLEM
-------------------------------
Signs the clog is in the main sewer line (requires professional service):
- Multiple drains backing up at the same time
- Gurgling sounds in the toilet when you run the sink
- Water backs up in the tub when you flush the toilet
- Sewage odor throughout the house
